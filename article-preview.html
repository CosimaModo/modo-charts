European BESS financing in 2025: deal count tripled as the market matured

The bottleneck has shifted. Grid connections and permitting now determine how fast Europe's BESS pipeline converts to megawatts on the ground.

Europe's BESS financing market grew faster in 2025 than in any previous year. Modo Energy tracked 77 financing deals across 13 countries. That is more than triple the 25 recorded in 2024. Disclosed debt rose from EUR 1.4 billion to EUR 5.0 billion. The lending base widened and tolling agreements spread. Institutional investors committed EUR 1.1 billion to platform-level BESS pipelines.

This is the first edition of Modo Energy's European BESS financing report. It covers all deals tracked in 2025. Future editions will provide quarterly updates.

Key takeaways

  • Deal count more than tripled from 25 to 77. Disclosed debt rose from EUR 1.4 billion to EUR 5.0 billion, driven by both higher volumes and larger individual transactions.
  • Standalone BESS accounted for 88% of deals, up from 63% in 2024. Co-located solar-plus-storage made up the remainder.
  • Germany led deal count with 25 transactions. Italy emerged as Europe's third most active market with 12 deals. The UK delivered Europe's largest BESS project finance close at 1.4 GW with Thorpe Marsh.
  • Standalone tolling agreements rose from 3 to 15. A further 4 project finance deals involved tolling counterparties, bringing the total to 19.
  • Merchant revenue underpinned 43% of deals. Markets with less trading history, such as Finland, relied entirely on contracted revenue. The pattern suggests merchant financing becomes accessible only as markets mature.

What types of deals does this report track?

Project finance covers all debt-raised capital used to fund construction or operation of a BESS project, including senior secured loans, leasing structures, and mezzanine facilities.

M&A covers sell- and buy-side transactions where a standalone or co-located BESS project is the primary asset, including full and partial asset and portfolio sales.

Equity investment covers all equity capital deployed into BESS projects.

Offtake agreement is a contract that secures future revenue for a BESS project, typically through a toll, floor or hybrid contract.

Deal value is the total disclosed financial value of a transaction, regardless of type. For project finance, this includes total debt raised. For M&A, this is the acquisition price. For equity, it is the capital deployed.

European BESS deal count tripled in 2025

Modo Energy tracked 77 deals in 2025 with a total disclosed value of EUR 7.5 billion. Activity accelerated through the year. H1 produced 21 deals. H2 delivered 56. Q4 alone accounted for 35 transactions, 45% of the annual total.

The second-half concentration suggests the market hit its stride as lenders grew more comfortable with BESS risk. Development pipelines also matured to financial close.

What types of deals dominated?

Project finance accounted for 36 deals (47%) vs 15 in 2024. Acquisitions were second at 21 (27%) vs 12 in 2024.

The growth of acquisitions alongside project finance points to a maturing secondary market. Developers are building, then selling operational assets to long-term holders.

Platform-level equity also scaled. Three deals committed EUR 1.1 billion. Partners Group led with a EUR 400 million equity commitment to Green Flexibility. Harmony Energy raised EUR 354 million in private capital. APG backed Return Energy with EUR 300 million. These commitments came from pension funds seeking exposure to multi-gigawatt pipelines rather than individual projects. In turn, this capital is expected to translate into project financing over the next 12 to 24 months.

Tolling agreements proliferated. Standalone tolling deals rose from 3 in 2024 to 15 in 2025, with 6 of 15 in Germany. A further 4 project finance deals involved tolling counterparties. In total, tolling featured in 19 transactions, making it the fastest-growing revenue structure in European BESS.

Germany and the UK led, but Italy emerged as Europe's third market

Germany led by deal count with 25 transactions (32%). The UK followed with 15 (19%). Italy emerged as the third most active market with 12 deals (16%), driven by a wave of acquisitions and new tolling agreements. France and the Netherlands each recorded five.

By capacity, the order reverses. The UK led at 5.5 GW, driven by Thorpe Marsh's 1.4 GW close. Germany followed at 4.8 GW.

The Netherlands had five deals, four at or above 300 MW. All four Dutch deals with disclosed duration were four-hour assets. This makes the Netherlands an outlier in a market still concentrated at two hours.

Italy's 12 transactions spanned six acquisitions, three project financings and two offtake agreements. ContourGlobal and Vexuvo's 1.5 GW portfolio acquisition was the largest. Aer Soléir closed EUR 180 million in project finance debt for its 250 MW Rondissone project and separately secured a tolling agreement with EGO Energy. Zelestra signed a 500 MW tolling deal with BKW.

Romania's emergence alongside Finland, Lithuania, and Greece signals that BESS financing is no longer a two-country story.

Germany: Europe's most active BESS financing market

Germany recorded 25 deals totalling 4.8 GW and EUR 2.0 billion in disclosed value. No other European market matched its diversity: 10 project financings, 8 acquisitions, 6 tolling agreements, and 1 equity investment.

The tolling market stood out. Germany went from one standalone tolling agreement in 2024 to six in 2025. Vattenfall, RWE, ENGIE, Alpiq, Shell, and DB Energie all signed offtake contracts with German BESS developers. Terralayr alone secured three separate tolling partners.

Germany's acquisition market also distinguishes it. Five acquisitions exceeded 300 MW. These include Luxcara's purchase of the 520 MW Waltrop project and Prime Capital's EUR 500 million acquisition of WBS Power's 500 MW Project Jupiter. International capital is buying into German BESS at scale.

Merchant exposure underpinned 52% of German deals. Tolling revenue backed 28%. As more capacity comes online, the balance between merchant and contracted revenue will shape developer returns.

Revenue models split along lines of market maturity

Revenue model preferences divided sharply by market maturity.

Merchant revenue underpinned 33 deals (43%), concentrated in the UK and Germany. The UK financed 60% of its deals on a merchant basis. Germany followed at 52%. In both markets, lenders have enough BESS operating history to underwrite merchant risk.

In contrast, markets with less trading history relied on contracted income. Finland's three deals were entirely tolling-backed. France secured offtake agreements for three of its five deals. The Netherlands favoured hybrid structures for three of five deals.

This pattern is consistent across the dataset: merchant financing follows operating track record. As newer markets accumulate performance data, their revenue models will likely shift towards merchant exposure.

Project sizes grew and longer durations gained ground recently

The median project size rose from 88 MW in 2024 to 125 MW in 2025 (excluding platform equity). The average held steady at around 230 MW. The median increase reflects a shift towards 200 to 500 MW projects, which became standard in 2025.

Duration remained concentrated at two hours. However, four-hour systems gained ground. GIGA Leopard, Lion Mufasa, and Sophocles all closed at four-hour configurations. Wholesale price spreads are widening in markets with high renewable penetration. Longer durations improve the revenue case for BESS.

Which lenders were most active?

Santander led by deal count with five European BESS transactions. NatWest followed with four. NordLB, ING, and BNP Paribas each participated in three.

The breadth of the lending base tells a bigger story than any single name. Thorpe Marsh's EUR 700 million debt package drew 13 banks including SMBC, China Minsheng, and Standard Chartered. Asian banks appearing in European BESS syndications for the first time signals that the asset class has crossed a threshold of institutional acceptance.

The tolling counterparty market expanded in parallel. Eighteen offtakers backed BESS projects in 2025, up from five in 2024.

What does 2025 tell us about 2026?

The financing market proved its readiness in 2025. Capital is available. The lending pool is deep. Institutional investors are committed. The question for 2026 is whether grid connection timelines and permitting processes can keep pace with development pipelines.

Three dynamics will shape the year ahead. Platform equity commitments totalling EUR 1.1 billion will convert into project financings. The geographic spread will continue widening as Italy, Romania, Finland, and Greece build on their 2025 activity. Germany's tolling market will test whether contracted revenue can scale fast enough to match its merchant-heavy pipeline.

The constraint has moved from capital to infrastructure.
