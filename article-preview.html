ARTICLE PREVIEW — MODO ENERGY

European BESS financing in 2025: deal count doubled as the market matured

Europe's BESS financing market grew faster in 2025 than in any previous year. Modo Energy tracked 68 deals across 13 countries, more than double the 30 recorded in 2024. The market also matured. The lending base broadened, tolling agreements proliferated in Germany, and new forms of institutional capital entered the sector.

This is the first edition of Modo Energy's European BESS financing report. It covers all deals tracked in 2025. Future editions will provide quarterly updates.

Key takeaways

  • Deal count more than doubled from 30 to 68. Disclosed debt rose from EUR 1.8 billion to EUR 4.2 billion, reflecting both higher deal volumes and larger individual transactions.
  • Standalone BESS accounted for 90% of deals, up from 63% in 2024. Co-located solar-plus-storage projects made up the remainder.
  • Germany accounted for 38% of deals with 26 transactions. The UK delivered the largest project finance close at 1.45 GW with Thorpe Marsh.
  • Standalone tolling agreements rose from three in 2024 to 11 in 2025. Six of the 11 were in Germany. An additional seven project finance and acquisition deals involved tolling counterparties.
  • Merchant revenue underpinned 41% of deals, concentrated in the UK and Germany. Tolling-backed revenue accounted for 22%. Hybrid structures combining merchant and contracted income made up 19%.

What types of deals does this report track?

Project finance covers all debt-raised capital used to fund the construction or operation of a BESS project, including senior secured loans, leasing structures, and mezzanine facilities.

M&A covers sell- and buy-side transactions where a standalone or co-located BESS project is the primary asset, including full and partial asset and portfolio sales.

Equity investment covers all equity capital deployed into BESS projects.

Offtake agreement is a contract that secures future revenue for a BESS project, typically through a fixed-price Flexibility Purchase Agreement (FPA).

Deal value is the total disclosed financial value of a transaction, regardless of type. For project finance, this includes total debt raised. For M&A, this is the acquisition price. For equity, this is the capital deployed.

How much BESS financing closed in Europe in 2025?

Sixty-eight deals closed in 2025, up from 30 in 2024.

Disclosed debt totalled EUR 4.2 billion, more than double the EUR 1.8 billion in 2024. Total disclosed deal value reached EUR 5.6 billion. This growth reflects both more deals closing and individual transactions getting larger.

Activity accelerated through the year. H1 produced 21 deals. H2 delivered 47. Q4 was the busiest quarter with 31 transactions, compared with 16 in Q3.

What types of deals dominated?

Project finance accounted for 36 of 68 deals (53%), up from 50% in 2024. Acquisitions were the second most common type at 18 deals (26%), up from 12 in 2024. The growth of acquisitions alongside project finance suggests a maturing secondary market for operational and later-stage BESS assets.

Platform-level equity investment grew substantially. Three deals in 2025 committed EUR 1.0 billion, led by Partners Group's EUR 400 million equity investment in Green Flexibility and APG's EUR 300 million backing of Return Energy. These commitments came from pension funds and private equity firms seeking exposure to multi-gigawatt BESS pipelines rather than individual projects.

Standalone tolling agreements rose from three in 2024 to 11 in 2025. Six of the 11 were in Germany. An additional seven project finance and acquisition deals involved tolling counterparties, bringing the total number of tolling-linked transactions to 18.

Which countries led European BESS financing?

Germany led by deal count with 26 transactions (38%). The UK followed with 16 (24%). No other country exceeded five.

By capacity, the UK led at 5.9 GW, driven by Thorpe Marsh's 1.45 GW close. Germany followed at 4.9 GW. Romania ranked third at 984 MW across three projects, led by the 534 MW Ogrezeni close.

The Netherlands had five deals, four at or above 300 MW. Finland and France each had smaller deal counts but meaningful projects backed by contracted revenue.

Revenue structures diverged by market maturity

Revenue model preferences varied sharply across countries.

Merchant revenue underpinned 28 deals (41%). Tolling-backed revenue accounted for 15 (22%). Hybrid structures made up 13 (19%). Twelve deals did not disclose their revenue model.

The UK, as Europe's most mature BESS market, financed 56% of its deals on a merchant basis. Germany followed at 50%. Germany's merchant share is likely to grow as installed BESS capacity increases and lenders gain further experience underwriting merchant risk.

In contrast, markets with less trading history relied more heavily on contracted revenue. Finland's three deals were entirely tolling-backed. France's two deals both secured offtake agreements. The Netherlands favoured hybrid structures for three of five deals. This pattern suggests that merchant-based financing becomes more accessible as markets mature.

Project sizes grew and longer durations gained ground

Excluding platform equity deals, the median BESS project size rose from 88 MW in 2024 to 130 MW in 2025. The average remained broadly stable at around 235 MW. The median increase reflects a shift towards 200-500 MW projects, which became increasingly common in 2025.

Duration remained concentrated at two hours. However, four-hour systems gained ground. GIGA Leopard, Lion Mufasa, and Sophocles all closed at four-hour configurations. The Netherlands stood out: all four deals with disclosed duration were four-hour assets.

Which lenders were most active?

Santander was the most active European BESS lender by deal count in 2025, appearing in five project finance transactions. NatWest followed with four deals. NordLB, ING, and BNP Paribas each participated in three. A further ten lenders each appeared in two deals.

The breadth of the lending base grew. Thorpe Marsh's EUR 690 million debt package drew 13 named banks including SMBC, China Minsheng, and Standard Chartered. This was the largest BESS lending syndicate assembled in Europe.

The tolling counterparty market also expanded. Sixteen offtakers backed BESS projects in 2025, up from five in 2024.

Germany: what made it Europe's busiest BESS market?

Germany recorded 26 deals totalling 4.9 GW and EUR 2.3 billion in disclosed value. No other European market matched its diversity. Eleven project financings, eight acquisitions, six tolling agreements, and one equity investment closed.

The tolling market stood out. Germany went from one standalone tolling agreement in 2024 to six in 2025. An additional acquisition, the Luxcara Waltrop deal, was backed by a tolling revenue model. Vattenfall, RWE, ENGIE, Alpiq, Shell, and DB Energie all signed offtake contracts with German BESS developers. Terralayr alone secured three separate tolling partners.

Germany's active acquisition market also distinguishes it from the rest of Europe. Five acquisitions exceeded 300 MW, including Luxcara's purchase of the 520 MW Waltrop project and Prime Capital's EUR 500 million acquisition of WBS Power's 500 MW Project Jupiter. This reflects a deep pool of development-stage assets changing hands as international capital seeks German BESS exposure.

Merchant exposure underpinned 50% of German deals. Tolling revenue backed 27%. As more German BESS capacity comes online, the balance between merchant and contracted revenue will be a key dynamic to watch.

What does 2025 tell us about 2026?

Three structural trends point to continued growth. The lending base widened from a handful of established names to a broad international pool. Platform equity commitments totalling EUR 1.0 billion will translate into individual project financings over the next 12 to 24 months. And the geographic spread is widening, with Romania, Finland, Lithuania, and Greece all appearing in 2025.

The constraint for 2026 may not be capital. The financing market has demonstrated its readiness. The question is whether grid connection timelines and permitting can keep pace with development pipelines.
